Syl Foisy

I'm a PhD candidate at NASA GISS and Columbia University studying aerosols — tiny liquid and solid particles in the atmosphere — which have an outsized influence on our climate, our weather, and the air we breathe. Using cutting-edge satellite datasets and global climate simulations, I work to understand their microphysical properties across the globe.

Outside of research, I have many interests, ranging from filmmaking and snow sculpture to long-distance walking, which occasionally, and delightfully, collide with my science.

About

Sky watcher.

I'm a PhD candidate at Columbia University / NASA GISS studying atmospheric aerosols — the tiny particles suspended in the air that scatter sunlight, seed clouds, and quietly steer Earth's climate.

My work uses satellite data from NASA's PACE mission and ground-based fieldwork to understand how aerosols behave, where they come from, and how we should represent them in the models we use to project our future climate. I'm advised by Dr. Susanne Bauer and Dr. Kostas Tsigaridis, and supported by a NASA FINESST Graduate Fellowship.

I believe science only matters if people understand it — so alongside my research, I make videos, build interactive explainers, and talk to anyone who will listen about what's happening in our atmosphere.

"During my undergraduate thesis at Williams College, I built a cloud chamber alongside Prof. Anthony Carrasquillo to study how aerosols respond to changes in humidity. Many undergo a striking transformation, separating into two distinct liquid phases, much like oil and water. These phase changes have real consequences for how aerosols scatter light, facilitate cloud formation, and shape climate. I'm proud that students at Williams continue this research today."

Aerosol properties & distribution

Understanding the physical and optical properties of aerosols — how they scatter, absorb, and interact with solar radiation. My work integrates retrievals from satellites and ground-based instruments to constrain these properties at scale.

NASA PACE SPEXone

Leveraging co-located data from NASA's PACE satellite — specifically the SPEXone polarimeter — alongside NASA ModelE-MATRIX to determine atmospheric composition and improve aerosol retrievals.

NASA PACE
🌍

Earth system modeling

Advancing NASA's CMIP-class climate model, GISS ModelE3.1, by integrating aerosol retrievals from satellites and ground-based observations — connecting real-world measurements to projections of our future climate.

NASA GISS ModelE

ModelE aerosol simulation — Aerosol composition across the globe for one week in April 2024, from a NASA GISS ModelE simulation. Each column of atmosphere is colored by its volume-dominant aerosol species.
